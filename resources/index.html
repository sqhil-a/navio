Resources

Useful questions and checklists for figuring out what to try next.

These free guides are designed for young people who want practical ways to explore careers, prepare for conversations, contribute to community, and reflect on their growth.

Guide 01

Questions to ask when exploring a career

Career research is more useful when you compare the everyday reality of a role, not only its title or salary.

  1. What kinds of problems does someone in this role solve in a normal week?
  2. Which tasks take the most time, and which are most energizing or difficult?
  3. What entry routes exist besides the most obvious one?
  4. Which skills are essential on day one, and which are learned on the job?
  5. What work settings, schedules, or physical demands are common?
  6. How is this field changing, and what might be different in five years?
  7. What could I try now-a course, project, conversation, club, or volunteer task-to test my interest?
Finish with:

“The next small experiment I can try is…”

Guide 02

How to prepare for a mentor conversation

A good conversation is not about impressing someone. It is about being prepared, curious, respectful, and specific.

Before

  • Read the person’s public professional background
  • Write down three questions you cannot answer through basic research
  • Prepare a 30-second introduction: who you are, what you are exploring, and why
  • Decide what you want to understand by the end

During

  • Listen fully before planning your next question
  • Ask for examples, tradeoffs, and lessons-not private details or a job
  • Take brief notes only if the person is comfortable

After

  • Send a concise thank-you
  • Write down one insight and one action
  • Do not assume ongoing access; ask before contacting them again

Guide 03

Choose meaningful extracurricular activities

The longest list is not always the strongest. Look for experiences that fit your capacity and teach you something you care about.

Interest

Am I curious enough to keep showing up after the first week?

Contribution

Who benefits, and will I have a real responsibility?

Growth

Which skill, perspective, or relationship could I develop?

Capacity

Can I meet the actual schedule without harming school, work, health, or family commitments?

A useful choice: fits your current life, offers a real way to contribute, and gives you something specific to learn.

Guide 04

Volunteer-readiness checklist

Volunteer service is a responsibility to people and organizations-not only a way to collect hours.

  • I understand the role, duties, schedule, location, and supervision.
  • I have confirmed age, consent, screening, and safety requirements.
  • I know who to contact if I am late, absent, unsure, or concerned.
  • I can protect confidential information and respect boundaries.
  • I have asked my school whether the role qualifies, if hours matter to me.
  • I know how completed service will be verified and documented.
  • I can commit without overloading my schedule.
  • I am willing to ask questions and receive feedback.

Guide 05

Beginner networking without the awkward script

Networking is the practice of building respectful professional relationships. It is not asking strangers to give you opportunities.

  1. Begin with context. Say where you met or what shared topic led you to reach out.
  2. Make a small request. Ask one focused question or request a brief conversation-not “Can you mentor me?”
  3. Respect the answer. No response or a “no” does not require repeated follow-up.
  4. Be specific. “What surprised you about your first year in this field?” is easier to answer than “Tell me everything.”
  5. Close the loop. Thank the person and, if appropriate, share how you used their advice.
Simple message example

Hello [Name], I heard you speak about [topic] at [event]. I’m exploring [area], and your point about [specific idea] stayed with me. If you have time, could I ask one question about [focused topic]? I understand if your schedule does not allow it. Thank you.

Guide 06

Leadership reflection questions

Leadership can happen without a title. It is visible in how you help a group understand the goal, make decisions, and follow through.

  • What did the group need, and how did I notice it?
  • Whose perspective was missing from the decision?
  • What did I communicate clearly? What caused confusion?
  • When did I take initiative, and when should I have asked first?
  • How did I respond when the plan changed?
  • What responsibility did I complete, not just start?
  • What feedback did I receive, and what will I do differently?

Guide 07

Event preparation checklist

Arriving prepared helps you focus on learning and participating instead of avoidable uncertainty.

Confirm

  • Date, start and end time, time zone, location, and format
  • Registration confirmation, cost, eligibility, and what to bring
  • Travel, accessibility, accommodation, consent, and emergency contact needs

Prepare

  • Read the event purpose and speaker or organization information
  • Write two questions and one goal for attending
  • Plan how you will take notes and what you will do if you need help

Reflect

  • What did I learn?
  • Who should I thank?
  • What is one next action-and when will I take it?

Use what helps

Start with one question, one conversation, or one small experiment.
